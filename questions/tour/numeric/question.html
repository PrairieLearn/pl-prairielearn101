For floating point numeric inputs, where the number of digits after the decimal place is specified, you can either:

  • enter more digits than are required (and then you won't have to round your answer),
  • or round (don't concatenate!) your answer so to the specified number of digits (following the rule: digits 0 through 4 round down, digits 5 through 9 round up).

For example, the answer to this question is 2/3 (but as a decimal, not a ratio).

This is a valid answer - supplying more digits than required:

0.6666666

This is a valid answer - rounding (not truncating!) to four digits after the decimal place:

0.6667

But, this answer (truncating to four digits after the decimal place) would not be valid:

0.6666

When you are working on a problem with multiple computations that build on one another, rounding errors can propagate and get bigger through the sequence of computations. To avoid this, you should always keep more decimal places in your computation than are expected in your submitted answer.
